\begin{abstract}
Proton beam therapy (PBT) concentrates dose in the sharp Bragg peak characteristic of hadron ionisation, sparing the tissue beyond it. While this sharpness is the technology's advantage, it demands careful planning and monitoring of the beam range, and in particular of the distal edge (the point where the beam stops beyond the tumour), since an error of a few millimetres on its estimation can underdose the tumour or overexpose a critical organ behind it. A viable \emph{in vivo} technique is to image the positron emitters, mostly $^{15}$O and $^{11}$C, produced along the beam path, inside the treatment room, either immediately or shortly after the irradiation. Open PET geometries allow in-beam measurement with essentially zero delay after irradiation. However, a delay of a few minutes in the start of the acquisition can be amply compensated by the use of an in-room full ring with large sensitivity and excellent energy and spatial resolution. 

Here, we propose the use of in-room PET scanners based on the CRYSP concept. The acronym, {\em CRYogenic Sensitive PET}, refers to the key innovation of these devices, based on the fact that suitable scintillators such as CsI and BGO dramatically increase their light yield at cryogenic temperatures. To quantify their performance we define a scenario that models the treatment of a head tumour. We apply a typical treatment dose, with a spread-out Bragg peak (SOBP) designed to deliver conformal coverage of the tumour. The resulting $\beta^+$ activity is then recorded by our CRYSP scanners. An erfc fit is performed on the image projection, from which the depth of the $50\%$ point (half-height) on the distal fall-off of the
activity, \Rc, is obtained. Next, the RMS variation of \Rc, $\sigma_R$, is quantified. We study the impact of the physics (defined by isotope decay and washout) and the scanner response (defined by the apparatus sensitivity, spatial and energy resolution). We consider CsI and BGO crystals, and several scanner configurations. For all cases studied, BGO offers an excellent performance, while CsI combines a very good performance with lower costs and higher material availability, making it also a suitable option.   
  

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Proton beam therapy (PBT)
exploits the favourable interaction properties of protons in matter, in
particular their finite range and the characteristic dose maximum in depth
known as the Bragg peak~\cite{Jaekel2020}. Thanks to the steep dose gradients
and the sharp distal fall-off, proton beams can achieve highly conformal
tumour coverage while better sparing the surrounding normal tissue in
comparison to conventional photon radiation~\cite{Jaekel2020}. Full
exploitation of these physical advantages in clinical practice, however,
requires accurate knowledge of the beam stopping position in the patient, the so-called distal edge.

The depth at which a proton beam stops---and therefore the position of the
distal dose edge---is determined by the stopping power of the tissue it
traverses. This stopping power, in turn,  is 
inferred from X-ray CT images through a semi-empirical calibration that maps the X-ray attenuation recorded
in each voxel onto stopping-power ratios relative to water~\cite{Paganetti2012}. 
Because this calibration is only approximate, it
introduces an intrinsic range uncertainty of up to about $3\%$, which
propagates directly into the predicted location of the distal edge; further
uncertainty arises from day-to-day variations in patient anatomy and
positioning~\cite{Paganetti2012}.  


\emph{In vivo} verification of the delivered range gives a direct check on the
stopping position and a means of tightening the margins that presently absorb
the range uncertainty~\cite{Paganetti2012}. Among the techniques investigated for \emph{in vivo} verification of the beam range,
positron-emission tomography (PET) is one of the most widely studied. When the
beam energy is above the threshold for nuclear interactions between the
incoming protons and the tissue nuclei, positron-emitting nuclei (e.g.
$^{11}$C, $^{15}$O) are produced along the beam path, and the back-to-back
$511\,\mathrm{keV}$ photons ensuing from positron annihilation can be detected
in coincidence, providing non-invasive information on the beam stopping
position~\cite{Bennett1978,Enghardt2004}. The irradiation-induced activity
exhibits a pronounced fall-off shortly before the Bragg peak, whose
correlation with the delivered dose can be exploited for range
monitoring~\cite{Parodi2018}. 


Because positron emitters are produced only where the protons are still above
the threshold for nuclear reactions, the $\beta^{+}$ activity profile falls off
slightly upstream of the dose Bragg peak.
%, as shown in
%Fig.~\ref{fig:dose_activity}. 
The range, however, can be recovered by relating the
activity edge to the dose edge.


The dominant emitters in the $\beta^{+}$ trail that follows the proton beam are
$^{15}$O (half-life $2$~min) and $^{11}$C ($20$~min). Their different lifetimes
set the time evolution of the signal: $^{15}$O
dominates the first minutes after irradiation and then decays quickly, while
$^{11}$C carries the activity at later times. The acquisition window therefore
fixes both the number of detected counts and the isotope composition.


PET verification has been realised in three acquisition geometries, set apart by
when and where the signal is collected~\cite{Shakirin2011,Kraan2015}. 

In
\emph{offline} imaging the patient is taken to a nearby PET/CT some
$15$--$30$~minutes after treatment, which gives the full angular coverage of a
closed ring. The delay, however, carries serious costs: (i) reduced sensitivity,
since the abundant $^{15}$O has all but vanished ten minutes after the
irradiation; (ii) significant, and hard to model, biological washout; and (iii) a
co-registration step, needed because the patient is set up afresh on a separate
scanner, so the measured activity and the treatment plan lie in different
coordinate frames. Co-registration matches the PET/CT to the planning CT, and the
residual misalignment, typically a few millimetres, maps directly into an
apparent shift of the range~\cite{Knopf2009,zhu2013}.

In \emph{in-beam} imaging the signal is collected
during and immediately after irradiation with the detectors at the treatment
position, which retains the short-lived isotopes and keeps the patient
stationary. In order to leave the beam path clear, in-beam PET has typically used two planar detector heads~\cite{Crespo2006,Ferrero2018}. While in-beam minimises the effect of radioactive decay and washout, and eliminates the need of co-registration, the price to pay is reduced angular coverage, which lowers dramatically the sensitivity and introduces limited-angle image
artefacts that grow away from the field centre~\cite{Crespo2006,Moglioni2022}. It also clutters the beam-delivery zone. 

 In \emph{in-room} imaging a dedicated
scanner stands beside the beam and the patient is imaged on the treatment couch
after a transfer time which can be minimised to about two minutes. The short delay minimises both the effect of radioactive decay and washout, while the full ring geometry eliminates artefacts, and, importantly, allows a short scan, minimising in-scan washout and patient movement. Since
the patient stays on the couch, the couch motion itself---a known rigid transform
read from its positioning system---aligns the plan and PET coordinate frames, so
the registration follows from this couch transform~\cite{Zhu2011,Min2013}.
Furthermore, the scanner sits at some distance from the beam, so it can be
operated in parallel with beam delivery, potentially improving patient
throughput.

The systematic limitations on the precision of distal-point determination using $\beta^+$ activity depend
on many factors in addition to the scanner and the acquisition mode (offline,
in-beam, in-room). They include the reaction cross
sections~\cite{Espana2011}, the modelling of biological
washout~\cite{Mizuno2003,parodi2008}, the type of tissue being imaged, and the
body region being treated~\cite{Knopf2009}. Results on head-and-neck sites are
promising~\cite{Knopf2009,Min2013}, while patient motion has so far limited the
precision reachable in lung tumours~\cite{Knopf2009}. The best estimator of the
distal edge is likewise still under study, and several approaches have been
tried~\cite{grogg2013}. A sigmoid/erfc fit that locates the \Rc\ point of the
activity image and relates it to the \Roc\ point of the dose\footnote{\Roc\ is the depth of the $80\%$ point on the
distal fall-off of the \emph{dose}, the standard clinical proxy for the beam
range, due to its robustness to range straggling; \Rc\ is the depth of the $50\%$ point (half-height) on the distal fall-off of the
\emph{activity}, its steepest and hence best-determined point. The two are
separated by an offset set by the beam and tissue model, whose determination is critical for the technique.
} is statistically
robust, but relating it with \Roc\ requires a well-tuned Monte Carlo description of the ion
beam~\cite{Espana2011}. This has motivated attempts to fit the distal edge
directly from the data, which carry their own systematics~\cite{grogg2013}.
Overall the problem remains open, and the heterogeneity of the systems used by
the different groups complicates the picture. Among the options, offline PET
imaging is disfavoured, since it amplifies {\em avoidable} effects such as
reduced ion activity and increased washout, and in-beam PET has so far been
confined to research systems rather than clinical use.

A large-sensitivity in-room PET offers a number of advantages that can help closing the existing gaps. Achieving a precise and fast movement of the bed from the beam to the scan is purely a mechanical engineering problem which can be addressed, in particular if planned ahead of the installation; high sensitivity, combined with high spatial and energy resolution, provides a very precise activity image, and the known and stable parameters of the scanner permit a precise simulation. High sensitivity also allows for a short scan, minimising effects of washout and patient movement. All those features combined offer an ideal scenario to devise a technique of distal edge verification with real clinical application.

Here, we propose an in-room PET scanner based on the CRYSP concept \cite{soleti_crysp}. The acronym, {\em CRYogenic Sensitive PET}, refers to the key innovation of these devices: suitable scintillators such as CsI and BGO dramatically increase their light yield at cryogenic temperatures, in exchange for a slower decay constant. 

To quantify the application of CRYSP scanners to PBT, we define a scenario that idealises one of the more significant applications of PBT, e.g.\ the treatment of a head tumour, specifically an ependymoma (Fig.~\ref{fig:tumour}). We define a phantom which models a patient's head, a uniform brain-tissue ellipsoid, and apply a typical treatment dose, with a SOBP beam designed to deliver conformal coverage of the tumour. The resulting $\beta^+$ activity is then recorded by our CRYSP scanners. An erfc fit is performed on the image projection, from which the \Rc\ edge is obtained.

Our objective here is to quantify the RMS variation of \Rc\ ($\sigma_R$) due to known factors. These include: a) the impact of the PET itself (sensitivity, AFOV, resolution); b) the impact of radioactive decay, for different start-of-scan and total-scan times; and c) the impact of washout. Effects (a) and (b) together set the ``no-washout'' $\sigma_R$ reported below, while (c) adds the washout cost.

Notice that we do not attempt to quantify the systematic uncertainties such as density variations, the fidelity of Monte Carlo modelling (cross sections in particular), and the imperfect knowledge of the washout model. These have been amply covered in the literature~\cite{Espana2011,Knopf2009,Mizuno2003}, and the overall impact they quantify is of the order of a few millimetres in the best cases~\cite{Knopf2009,Paganetti2012}. Instead, our goal is to show that $\sigma_R$ measured with CRYSP scanners is small compared with these uncertainties, and thus the scanners offer an excellent tool to further quantify and reduce them in a controlled environment, and, eventually, a tool for clinical diagnosis.

\section{CRYSP scanners}
\label{sec:crysp}
Pure CsI is a fast but faint scintillator at room temperature,
with a light yield of about $5\,000$ photons/MeV and a decay constant of
$\sim\!15$~ns. Upon cooling to cryogenic temperatures ($\sim\!77$~K) its light
yield increases by a factor of about 20, reaching roughly
$1\times10^{5}$~photons/MeV, while the decay constant lengthens by a factor of
about 50 to $\sim\!800$~ns~\cite{soleti_crysp}. A similar behaviour is found
in BGO: recent measurements with a SiPM-coupled crystal yield an intrinsic
light output of about $1.4\times10^{4}$~photons/MeV at dry-ice temperature
($195$~K) and about $2.9\times10^{4}$~photons/MeV at liquid-nitrogen
temperature ($77$~K), to be compared with $\sim\!9\times10^{3}$~photons/MeV at
room temperature, again at the expense of a markedly slower response, with the
decay constant growing from $\sim\!300$~ns at $300$~K to about $1.8$~$\mu$s at
$195$~K and to a two-component decay of $\sim\!3$--$9$~$\mu$s at
$77$~K~\cite{ding_bgo}. The relevant figures are summarised in
Table~\ref{tab:cryo_scint}.

 In both cases, the intense light emission compensates the relatively slow decay constant, allowing a coincidence time resolution (CTR) of the order of a few nanoseconds. The long integration time needed to measure the signal, however, has a real impact on conventional PET imaging. As discussed in~\cite{soleti_crysp}, the impact of pile-up in a CsI scanner is modest and can be further reduced with dedicated electronics. This may also be the case for a BGO scanner operating at 195 K but pile-up in the case of BGO at 77 K is too high for typical high-activity medical imaging. However, the activity in PBT scans, even at large doses, is two to three orders of magnitude smaller than that of imaging applications (Figure \ref{fig:activity_iso}). This, combined with the delay time ($\sim 2$ minutes) until the start of the acquisition, results in no significant pile-up for CsI or BGO at 195~K and only a very small effect ($\sim 1\%$) for BGO at 77~K. Thus, the relatively slow scintillation response of these devices---the main limitation for clinical PET imaging---becomes a non-issue for PBT.
 
 In exchange, the large light yield has several benefits. First, it brings the energy resolution of the scanners essentially to the Fano limit. In CsI, a resolution of 6\% has been measured~\cite{soleti_csi}, and BGO can likely reach a similar value with enough light yield, though we adopt a conservative $10\%$ for it in this study. Excellent energy resolution has the desirable effect of reducing significantly the impact of multiple scattering in image reconstruction, thus minimising one important source of smearing in the image. Second, the use of monolithic crystals read out by SiPMs and reconstructing the three coordinates of the impinging gamma becomes feasible. There is enough light to guarantee excellent sharing between large SiPMs (typically of $6 \times 6$ mm$^2$, as proposed in~\cite{soleti_crysp}), and the use of convolutional neural networks allows an excellent spatial resolution in the three coordinates, thus eliminating parallax effects. Spatial resolution and no-parallax, in turn, contribute to a clean, artefact-free reconstruction of the image.

 The scanners also offer a very competitive cost. CsI is the cheapest option, and readily available. BGO, on the other hand, although a common material for PET, with a very competitive cost when compared with LYSO, has the drawback of restricted supply---an important fraction of the production comes from China---and more volatility in cost. In both cases, monolithic crystals reduce costs relative to their pixelated analogues. Furthermore, the readout is considerably cheaper. In large quantities the cost of the SiPMs is proportional to the deployed surface and independent of pixel size. The number of electronics channels, by contrast, does depend on it. CRYSP crystals are read out by matrices of $8 \times 8$ SiPMs of $6 \times 6$ mm$^2$, while conventional scanners use a typical size for pixels of  $3 \times 3$ mm$^2$. Thus the number of channels in a conventional scanner is of the order of a factor 4 larger than those of a CRYSP scanner. Finally, there is the cost of the electronics channels themselves. LYSO scanners use fast electronics and synchronisation between system clocks in the range of hundreds of picoseconds (to take advantage of TOF capabilities) while CRYSP scanners need to handle large signals with synchronisation at the nanosecond level. Typical costs of TOF electronics for LYSO scanners range around 20 \euro/channel, while the estimated cost for CRYSP electronics is of the order of 12 \euro/channel. The unit costs of the individual detector elements are collected in Table~\ref{tab:element-costs}, and Figure~\ref{fig:cost} summarises the resulting scanner cost.
 
An important factor not fully captured by the price tag is that of material availability. LYSO relies on lutetium, a rare earth, and BGO on germanium and bismuth; all are strategically controlled materials whose supply is concentrated in a few actors, China in particular. This introduces uncertainties on the supply (and variability of cost) absent in the case of CsI, a common and readily available material. 

Importantly for this application, monolithic crystals of CsI(Tl) are also readily available and offer a performance similar to that of cryogenic pure CsI in both light yield (50,000 photons/MeV, a factor two less than pure CsI at 77 K but still plenty for good energy and spatial resolution), and lifetime (also of the order of 1 $\mu$s). Like cryogenic BGO, the use of CsI(Tl) is problematic in high-activity applications due to its afterglow, but this effect is irrelevant in the low-activity environment of PBT.

Our setup is chosen to model one of the most relevant applications of PBT. Ependymomas are rare tumours of the central nervous system which arise from the ependymal cells lining the cerebral ventricles and
the central canal of the spinal cord~\citep{donahue2022,macdonald2013}. In children they are the third most common neuroepithelial tumour,
comprising roughly \SIrange{5}{10}{\percent} of childhood brain tumours, or about \num{3.4} cases per million children in Europe, aged \numrange{0}{14} years~\citep{peris2006},  with a median age at diagnosis
of only \SIrange{4}{6}{\years}. In children, ependymomas grow in the lower-back part of the skull, specifically in the cavity that holds the brainstem and cerebellum, and tend to be very aggressive (Figure \ref{fig:tumour}). Thus the need to adjust the beam to fully cover the tumour, while sparing healthy cerebellum tissue. This general requirement is all the more poignant in children and translates into the need to predict the distal edge with great accuracy. A target value often quoted in the literature is $\sim 1$ mm. 

At the same time, ependymomas in adults appear predominantly as a spinal-cord tumour, with about \SI{64}{\percent} arising in the spine~\citep{mcguire2009}. 


\subsection{The scanner}
The wide range of tumours that can be treated with a proton beam drives the CRYSP configurations that we have selected for this study, described below. 

We take as a reference a ``total body PET'' (TBP) with an
inner radius $R_{\mathrm{inner}} = \SI{387}{\milli\metre}$ and an axial length of
\SI{1024}{\milli\metre}, following the model presented in \cite{soleti_crysp}. The scanner deploys monolithic CsI or BGO crystals of $48 \times 48$~mm$^2$ surface and a depth of 2 radiation lengths
(2~X$_0$). It has 20 wheels and 48 crystals
per wheel, for a total of 960 crystals. The crystals and the SiPMs operate inside a cryostat. Signals are extracted through flat kapton cables through suitable feedthroughs and read with custom electronics. The operation temperature is  \SI{77}{\kelvin}
 (liquid nitrogen), with two important qualifications. It is possible to operate a scanner of CsI(Tl) at room temperature, and BGO can be operated at \SI{195}{\kelvin} instead of \SI{77}{\kelvin}, trading a factor two in light yield by a much shorter scintillation time. 
 
 In addition to the reference scanner, we consider two more configurations per crystal: a ``large AFOV'' (LAFOV) configuration with an axial length of \SI{500}{\milli\metre} (same diameter as the reference scanner), and a ``compact AFOV'' (CAFOV) configuration with an axial length of \SI{350}{\milli\metre}. The energy resolution of the scanners at \SI{511}{\kilo\electronvolt} is 6\% for CsI~\cite{soleti_crysp,soleti_csi} and
10\% for BGO---a conservative value given the high light yields, but still two times better than the results often quoted in the literature. The spatial resolution in the three
coordinates is \SI{3.5}{\milli\metre} FWHM~\cite{soleti_crysp}.


If the only aim were to verify proton treatments of tumours of the brain and
skull base, a compact configuration would be sufficient: the
target always sits in the same place, and a short axial field of view (AFOV)
covers it comfortably. The case for a larger scanner, on the other hand,  is its flexibility, e.g., the ability to deploy a single
instrument across the range of anatomical sites that proton therapy treats.
The CAFOV configuration is adequate for the great majority of
indications. Head and skull-base targets, and the localised deep-trunk targets
(prostate, pancreas, liver, kidney), are each smaller than \SI{35}{\centi\metre}
and are imaged by centring the region of interest in the scanner. The LAFOV (or even the TBP), on the other hand,
may be ideally suited for craniospinal irradiation spanning the whole neuraxis over roughly \SIrange{60}{70}{\centi\metre}. Of course, the longer axis also buys additional sensitivity, increasing the geometrical acceptance. The tradeoff is the cost, illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:cost}.

\subsection{Simulations}
Each simulated study corresponds to a dose of \SI{1}{\gray}. The parameters of the scan are $t_d$ (the delay, relative to the start of the dose, at which the scan starts) and $t_s$ (the scan duration). Each scan records
the list of lines of response (LORs) produced by a PET scanner. 
MLEM~\cite{shepp_vardi} is used to reconstruct the activity image
$A(x,y,z)$. \Rc\ is estimated through a depth-profile extraction, followed by an erfc fit.  

To find $\sigma_R$, ten independent ``shards'' (each of \SI{1}{\gray}) are run and pooled into a single event sample; the thinning method (Sec.~\ref{sec:thining}) is then executed over this pool to estimate $\sigma_R$.

The calibration offset $\DR = \Roc - \Rc$ between the activity midpoint \Rc\ and
the dose edge \Roc\ is fixed for a given setup and is determined beforehand (for
example, via a precise simulation of the setup); on real data one then fits \Rc\
and adds \DR\ to recover the distal edge \Roc\ (Sec.~\ref{sec:estimator}).

\section{The estimator}
\label{sec:estimator}


\begin{figure}[htbp!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/recon_projections_bgo.png}
  \caption{Orthogonal projections of the reconstructed activity of one
    \SI{1}{\gray} BGO run (transaxial, coronal, sagittal). The dashed curve
    is the phantom outline; the dotted line marks the fitted \Rc.}
  \label{fig:projections}
\end{figure}


Figure~\ref{fig:projections} shows orthogonal projections of one reconstructed
run. The depth profile is the activity integrated over the full transverse
plane at each depth,
%
\begin{equation}
  P(z) \;=\; \iint A(x,y,z)\,\mathrm{d}x\,\mathrm{d}y ,
  \label{eq:profile}
\end{equation}
%
which makes the profile insensitive to the transverse spreading of the beam
with depth. The distal fall-off is fitted with a complementary error
function plus a baseline,
%
\begin{equation}
  P(z) \;=\; b + \frac{a}{2}\,
  \operatorname{erfc}\!\left(\frac{z - z_0}{\sqrt{2}\,w}\right),
  \label{eq:erfc}
\end{equation}
%
inside a fixed window bracketing the edge ($\SI{-36.5}{mm} \le z \le \SI{-1.5}{mm}$
for the nominal beam), with Poisson weights. The four parameters are the
baseline $b$, the plateau amplitude $a$, the midpoint $z_0$ and the edge
width $w$. From this definition it follows that $\Rc \equiv z_0$. 
Figure~\ref{fig:fits} shows the fit on one run of each reference scanner.

\begin{figure}[htbp!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{figs/fit_all_events_bgo.png}
  \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{figs/fit_all_events_csi.png}
  \caption{Whole-plane depth profile of one reconstructed \SI{1}{\gray} run
    (points, Poisson errors)  with the erfc fit (Eq.~\ref{eq:erfc}, red line) in the
    fixed window (shaded). Left: BGO; right: CsI. The dashed guides
    mark the fitted plateau and its half; \Rc\ is the depth at which
    the fit crosses the half-height (dotted line, value in the legend).}
  \label{fig:fits}
\end{figure}

Notice that, for a given setup, the offset $\DR = \Roc - \Rc$ is fixed. A fit to
the data that determines \Rc, together with an estimate of \DR, therefore yields
\Roc\ and hence the proton distal edge. The uncertainty on \Roc\ can then be
expressed as


\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{\Roc} \;=\; \sigma_{\DR} \oplus \sigma_{\Rc},
  \label{eq:sigma_budget}
\end{equation}

The two terms are of a different nature. $\sigma_{\Rc}$ is obtained from the fit
to the data, so it is set by the errors on the measured activity profile $P(z)$.
These quantify the impact of the scanner---the sensitivity, which fixes the
available statistics; the energy resolution, which controls the effect of
multiple scattering; and the spatial resolution, which sets the precision with
which $P(z)$ is measured---together with radioactive decay and biological
washout, both of which reduce the activity with time. $\sigma_{\DR}$, in
contrast, quantifies the systematic error from an incomplete knowledge of the
setup: non-uniformities in the tissue that are not captured by the CT, the
imperfect knowledge of the nuclear cross sections, and the uncertainties in the
biological washout models, among others. For the past two decades the field has
worked to quantify and reduce these systematics, with a target of the order of
\SI{1}{mm}~\cite{Knopf2009,Paganetti2012}.

The target splits into two clinically distinct regimes. As a \emph{screening} tool---catching gross delivery errors such as a
wrong energy layer or a large anatomical change---a useful error is comparable to
the safety margin itself, of order $3$--$5$~mm, a level current PET systems already reach. 
The more demanding goal is \emph{margin
reduction}: the distal margin used in the clinic is
$M \simeq 3.5\%\times R + 1\,\mathrm{mm}$, about \SI{4.5}{mm} for a \SI{10}{cm}
range, and shrinking it safely calls for a total error at or below \SI{1}{mm}.  A useful criterion
is then to require $\sigma_{\Rc}$ to be negligible against the target,
$\sigma_{\Rc} \ll \SI{1}{mm}$, so that the useful error is limited by the
systematics rather than by the measurement. Driving $\sigma_{\Rc}$ well below the
millimetre is precisely the role of a high-sensitivity, high-resolution in-room
scanner, which then also becomes an instrument to isolate and, ultimately,
reduce $\sigma_{\DR}$ in a controlled setting. The remainder of this work
quantifies $\sigma_{\Rc}$ for the CsI and BGO scanners.

\section{Modelling biological washout}
\label{sec:washout}

The positron emitters produced along the beam path do not remain at their
production site. Perfusion and metabolism clear the radioactive atoms from the
irradiated volume, so the recorded activity is lower than physical decay alone
would predict. Following Mizuno et al.~\cite{Mizuno2003}, biological washout in
brain tissue is described by three exponential components (fast, medium and
slow), tentatively associated with direct blood flow, tissue--vessel exchange,
and trapping by stable molecules. The fraction of produced nuclei still present
a time $t$ after their creation is
%
\begin{equation}
  W(t) \;=\; \sum_{k=f,m,s} M_k\, e^{-\lambda_k^{\mathrm{bio}}\,t},
  \qquad \sum_{k} M_k = 1,
  \qquad \lambda_k^{\mathrm{bio}} = \frac{\ln 2}{T_k^{\mathrm{bio}}},
  \label{eq:washout}
\end{equation}
%
with the brain parameters $M_k$ and $T_k^{\mathrm{bio}}$ of
Table~\ref{tab:washout_brain}.

\begin{table}[ht]
  \centering
  \begin{tabular}{lccc}
    \hline
    Component & Fraction $M_k$ & $T_k^{\mathrm{bio}}$ (s) &
      $\lambda_k^{\mathrm{bio}}$ (s$^{-1}$) \\
    \hline
    Fast   & $0.35\,(3)$ & $2.0\,(1.8)$    & $3.5\times10^{-1}$ \\
    Medium & $0.30\,(3)$ & $140\,(18)$     & $5.0\times10^{-3}$ \\
    Slow   & $0.35\,(1)$ & $10191\,(2200)$ & $6.8\times10^{-5}$ \\
    \hline
  \end{tabular}
  \caption{Three-component biological washout parameters for brain tissue,
  from Mizuno et al.~\cite{Mizuno2003}. Uncertainties on the last digits are
  in parentheses.}
  \label{tab:washout_brain}
\end{table}

The productions available to us model physical decay only, storing per species
$i$ the window-integrated signal without washout. Combining
Eq.~(\ref{eq:washout}) with the physical decay of species $i$ (rate
$\lambda_i^{\mathrm{phys}}$) and integrating over the acquisition window
$[t_1,t_2]$ opened after a uniform irradiation of duration $t_{\mathrm{irr}}$,
the whole effect of washout on the recorded signal of species $i$ reduces to a
single survival factor $g_i$, the fraction of physically recorded $i$-decays
that also survive clearance,
%
\begin{equation}
  g_i \;=\; \sum_{k=f,m,s} M_k\,
  \frac{\Phi\!\left(\lambda_i^{\mathrm{phys}}+\lambda_k^{\mathrm{bio}}\right)}
       {\Phi\!\left(\lambda_i^{\mathrm{phys}}\right)},
  \qquad
  \Phi(a) \;=\;
  \frac{\bigl(e^{a\,t_{\mathrm{irr}}}-1\bigr)
        \bigl(e^{-a t_1}-e^{-a t_2}\bigr)}{a^{2}} .
  \label{eq:gfactor}
\end{equation}
%
The production rate, spatial density and normalisation cancel in the ratio, so
$g_i$ is a pure number per isotope, and for a spatially uniform clearance it is
independent of position. 
Because each $g_i$ multiplies its species' entire depth profile $P_i(z)$ by a
constant, washout distorts no single isotope's shape and acts purely by
re-weighting the mixture,
%
\begin{equation}
  P^{\mathrm{wo}}(z) \;=\; \sum_i g_i\,P_i(z),
  \qquad
  P^{0}(z) \;=\; \sum_i P_i(z).
  \label{eq:mix}
\end{equation}
%
The endpoint \Rc\ moves only insofar as the species occupy different depths.
$^{15}$O, with the lowest production threshold, reaches deepest and owns the
distal edge, while $^{11}$C lies shallower. Every recorded nucleus is older than
the acquisition delay, well beyond the \SI{2}{\second} fast scale, so the fast component
is spent for all species and washout roughly halves the signal. The short-lived
$^{15}$O, whose decays concentrate at small age, is suppressed less than the long-lived
$^{11}$C, so the deep-edge contributor is relatively preserved.



\section{Statistical treatment of the data: thinning}
\label{sec:thining}

The range precision $\sigma_R$, the run-to-run spread of \Rc\ at a given dose, is
a statistical quantity, so it must be estimated from an ensemble of acquisitions.
Generating that ensemble by direct simulation is expensive, since each member is
a full Monte Carlo plus reconstruction, and the productions supply only a handful
of independent realisations (``shards''). Thinning turns a small pool of shards
into a large ensemble, and through the same operation applies the dose, the
acquisition-start cut, and the washout.

Let there be $n$ statistically independent shards, each a complete acquisition at
the reference dose ($1$~Gy) produced with an independent seed. Pool them into one
master list of $M$ coincidences, physically $n$~Gy of events. A thinned
realisation keeps each event $e$ independently with probability $q_e$: draw
$u_e\sim\mathcal{U}[0,1)$ and keep $e$ if $u_e<q_e$. The kept subset is
reconstructed exactly as a real acquisition, yielding one \Rc. Repeating with
independent draws $N$ times gives $N$ endpoints, and
%
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_R \;=\; \mathrm{std}\bigl(R_{50}^{(1)},\dots,R_{50}^{(N)}\bigr),
  \qquad
  \frac{\delta\sigma_R}{\sigma_R}\;\simeq\;\frac{1}{\sqrt{2(N-1)}}.
  \label{eq:sigmaR}
\end{equation}
%
Everything specific to a study lives in the keep-probability, which factorises
into independent effects,
%
\begin{equation}
  q_e \;=\; \underbrace{\mathbf{1}\!\left[t_{\mathrm{dec}}(e)\ge t_d\right]}
              _{\text{acquisition start}}
        \;\times\; \underbrace{D/n}_{\text{dose}}
        \;\times\; \underbrace{w\bigl(z_0(e),t_{\mathrm{dec}}(e)\bigr)}
              _{\text{washout}} ,
  \label{eq:keep}
\end{equation}
%
where $z_0(e)$ and $t_{\mathrm{dec}}(e)$ are the event's origin depth and decay
time, both recorded per coincidence. The first factor drops decays occurring
before the scan opens at delay $t_d$. The second keeps a fraction $D/n$ of the
$n$-Gy pool, producing $D$~Gy of events and letting $\sigma_R(D)$ be mapped
without new simulation, with $\sigma_R\propto 1/\sqrt{D}$. The third applies
washout as a per-event loss through the survival $w$, which reduces to the
species' own $g_i$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:washout}) where the emitting isotope is known,
as it is in the production Monte Carlo. Setting the washout factor to unity gives
the nominal case, and replacing it by a single species' abundance selects an
``$i$-like'' sub-sample used to measure the per-species precision.

The method rests on the thinning theorem for Poisson processes. Radioactive
production is Poisson, and independent Bernoulli thinning of a Poisson process is
again Poisson with scaled intensity, so in the large-pool limit a thinned
realisation is a genuine lower-dose acquisition carrying its correct counting
noise. 
Given the fixed pool, realisations built from independent draws are mutually
independent, so the $N$ endpoints are $N$ independent samples of the \Rc\
distribution and Eq.~(\ref{eq:sigmaR}) carries genuine $N$-sample precision:
$\pm10\%$ at $N=50$, $\pm5\%$ at $N=200$.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclu}

CRYSP scanners offer an excellent alternative to in-beam scanners, thanks to their full ring geometry, high sensitivity, excellent energy and spatial resolution and low cost. Their use affords short scans that minimise the impact of systematic uncertainties related to nuclear cross-sections~\cite{Espana2011}, biological washout~\cite{Mizuno2003} or patient movement~\cite{Knopf2009}. The intrinsic uncertainty introduced by the scanner design (combined with radioactive decay and including a model for washout) is much smaller than the sought precision for distal-edge prediction. Several configurations are possible, depending on the application and the budget. The optimal solution for a fully flexible scanner with very high precision may be the BGO LAFOV, but CsI CAFOV also offers an excellent case, in particular for head and well-localised tumours, at a very competitive cost. The systematic use of these scanners may be a crucial step to reach a high precision prediction on the distal edge of the treatment.
