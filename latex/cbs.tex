\begin{abstract}
Proton therapy concentrates dose in the sharp Bragg peak characteristic of hadron ionisation, sparing the tissue beyond it. While this sharpness is the technology's advantage, it demands careful planning and monitoring of the beam range, and in particular of the distal edge (the point where the beam stops beyond the tumour), since an error of a few millimetres on its estimation can underdose the tumour or overexpose a critical organ behind it. A viable {\em in vivo} technique  is to image the positron emitters, mostly $^{15}$O and $^{11}$C, produced along the beam path, inside the treatment room, either immediately or shortly after the irradiation. Open PET geometries allow in-beam measurement with essentially zero delay after irradiation. Here we argue, however, a delay of a few minutes in the start of the acquisition can be amply compensated by the use of an in-room full ring with a long axial field of view and excellent energy and spatial resolution. 

Specifically we propose the CRYSP (CRYogenic Sensitive PET) technology, based in the use of large, cooled monolithic crystals. We show that a large (1 m AFOV) in-room scanner applying this technology allows a precise distal edge determination at low proton doses, and is thus suitable as a precise verification instrument in proton therapy. Importantly, long AFOV CRYSP scanners allow verification of proton treatment in any region of the body (as opposed to specialised head-scanners, for example) and can be built as dedicated devices at an affordable cost.

Monolithic CsI provides high sensitivity at low cost, resolves the depth of interaction, removing parallax, and reaches an energy resolution that rejects the scattered background through a narrow window around 511 keV. Because the measurement is made at low rate after the beam, the detector is free of pile-up, and the crystal can be optimised for energy resolution regardless of decay time.

\section{Introduction}


\section{Introduction}

Proton therapy
exploits the favourable interaction properties of protons in matter, in
particular their finite range and the characteristic dose maximum in depth
known as the Bragg peak~\cite{Jaekel2020}. Thanks to the steep dose gradients
and the sharp distal fall-off, proton beams can achieve highly conformal
tumour coverage while better sparing the surrounding normal tissue in
comparison to conventional photon radiation~\cite{Jaekel2020}. Full
exploitation of these physical advantages in clinical practice, however,
requires accurate knowledge of the beam stopping position in the patient, the so-called distal edge.

The typical medical treatment-planning workflow relies on a semi-empirical calibration
of X-ray computed tomography (CT) images into stopping-power ratios relative
to water, which can introduce intrinsic range uncertainties of up to about
$3\%$, in addition to unavoidable uncertainties in the daily patient anatomy
and positioning~\cite{Paganetti2012}. These uncertainties translate into the
conservative use of safety margins around the target volume and the avoidance
of beam directions stopping just in front of radiosensitive organs. Reducing
range uncertainties at the planning or delivery stage therefore remains an open problem~\cite{Paganetti2012,Parodi2018}.

Among the techniques investigated for in-vivo verification of the beam range,
positron-emission tomography (PET) is one of the most widely studied. When the
beam energy is above the threshold for nuclear interactions between the
incoming protons and the tissue nuclei, positron-emitting nuclei (e.g.
$^{11}$C, $^{15}$O) are produced along the beam path, and the back-to-back
$511\,\mathrm{keV}$ photons ensuing from positron annihilation can be detected
in coincidence, providing non-invasive information on the beam stopping
position~\cite{Bennett1978,Enghardt2004}. The irradiation-induced activity
exhibits a pronounced fall-off shortly before the Bragg peak, whose
correlation with the delivered dose can be exploited for range
monitoring~\cite{Parodi2018}. 

The first extensive clinical demonstration of PET-based range monitoring was
achieved with in-beam acquisition, exploiting a dedicated, limited-angle
dual-head scanner able to operate during dose delivery~\cite{Enghardt2004}.
Building on early phantom and animal studies with planar cameras, this
approach showed that the method could monitor the beam range with millimetres
accuracy at typical therapeutic doses, detect delivery errors caused by
anatomical changes, patient set-up errors or incorrect CT calibration, and
even support dose quantification from the measured
activity~\cite{Enghardt2004,Enghardt2004b}. These results established in-beam
PET as an important contribution to treatment verification. Their obvious advantages are that they
minimise the loss
of signal from isotope decay and biological washout, as well as the co-registration issues that affect
delayed measurements.

The performance of these pioneering systems was, however, intrinsically
limited by their open geometry and reduced angular acceptance. To leave an
accessible path for the beam and the patient, the detectors were arranged as
two opposing heads covering only a small fraction of the full solid angle,
which results in missing projection views, limited-angle reconstruction
artifacts and a corresponding degradation of spatial resolution along the
axis perpendicular to the detector planes~\cite{Enghardt2004,Crespo2006}.
These effects are most pronounced for large tumour volumes in extra-cranial
locations, constraining the achievable image quality independently of the
detector technology employed.

Partly to circumvent these geometrical limitations and the difficulty of
in-beam acquisition at continuous-wave accelerators, most subsequent clinical
investigations adopted  off-room 
implementations based on commercial PET(/CT) scanners originally designed for
nuclear medicine~\cite{Shakirin2011,Crespo2005}. While these full-ring systems
offer broad availability and favourable geometry, they are not optimised for
the specific requirements of range monitoring: reconstruction of the very
weak, broad and multi-isotope activity distributions produced during
irradiation presents a different set of problems than the reconstruction of localised, intense single-isotope
sources typical of radiotracer imaging. Furthermore, the additional delay introduced to move the patient exacerbates
biological washout~\cite{Knopf2009,Parodi2018}. As a consequence, accuracy
studies comparing measured activity with Monte Carlo predictions have
typically remained limited to an agreement of a few mm, generally
regarded as still unsatisfactory for routine clinical exploitation and
motivating the development of detectors tailored to the
problem~\cite{Parodi2018}.
 
While a new generation of in-beam detectors are currently being developed, an alternative in-between in-beam and off-room 
approaches is in-room optimised scanners. 

In this paper, we show that CRYSP scanners are an optimal solution for in-room measurements in proton therapy. The acronym, CRYogenic Sensitive PET, refers to the crucial innovation of these devices, based in the fact that suitable scintillators such as CsI and BGO increase dramatically their light yield at cryogenic temperatures, in exchange of a slower decay constant. Specifically, pure CsI is a fast but faint scintillator at room temperature,
with a light yield of about $5\,000$ photons/MeV and a decay constant of
$\sim\!15$~ns. Upon cooling to cryogenic temperatures ($\sim\!100$~K) its light
yield increases by a factor of about 20, reaching roughly
$1\times10^{5}$~photons/MeV, while the decay constant lengthens by a factor of
about 50 to $\sim\!800$~ns~\cite{soleti_crysp}. A similar behaviour is found
in BGO: recent measurements with a SiPM-coupled crystal yield an intrinsic
light output of about $1.4\times10^{4}$~photons/MeV at dry-ice temperature
($195$~K) and about $2.9\times10^{4}$~photons/MeV at liquid-nitrogen
temperature ($77$~K), to be compared with $\sim\!9\times10^{3}$~photons/MeV at
room temperature, again at the expense of a markedly slower response, with the
decay constant growing from $\sim\!300$~ns at $300$~K to about $1.8$~$\mu$s at
$195$~K and to a two-component decay of $\sim\!3$--$9$~$\mu$s at
$77$~K~\cite{ding_bgo}. The relevant figures are summarised in
Table~\ref{tab:cryo_scint}.

 In the case of CsI, the intense light emission compensates the relatively slow decay constant, allowing a coincidence time resolution (CTR) of \SI{1.5}{ns}. As discussed in ~\cite{soleti_crysp}, a LAFOV CRYSP detector based in CsI, offers a competitive performance in ordinary PET applications at a much reduced cost, both in terms of the crystals themselves (CsI crystals can be a factor ten cheaper than LYSO crystals) and the reduced number of electronics channels associated to the possibility of using large SiPMs (typically $6 \times 6$ mm$^2$ instead of $3 \times 3$ mm$^2$ often used in pixelated scanners) for the readout of the very luminous CsI monolithic crystals. 
 
Furthermore, the timing features of a CsI CRYSP scanners do not have any impact in the performance of the device for in-room proton therapy applications. Instead, its large AFOV affords a high angular coverage, the uniform resolution in the three coordinates avoid parallax errors at large angles, and its excellent energy resolution minimises the impact of multiple scattering. While CsI has lower density ($\mathrm{4.5~g/cm^3}$) than BGO ($\mathrm{7.1~g/cm^3}$), and a longer radiation length ($\mathrm{1.86 cm}$ versus $\mathrm{1.12 cm}$), if offers better light yield output and CTR, as well as a more competitive cost, and ---crucially--- guaranteed supply. While CsI is a common material, readily available at stable cost, the BGO uses rare metals, whose cost can spike in unpredictable way and whose supply is much more restricted. 
 
 However, it is worth to evaluate the performance of cryogenic BGO in in-room proton therapy applications, given its high density, short radiation length and high photoelectric cross section. 





CryspBrainSim (CBS) fits the activity distribution of the $\beta^+$ emitters
produced in the decay of the various isotopes activated by a proton beam crossing
(brain) tissue and delivering a conformal dose into a (head) tumour. The case
chosen as representative is an ependymoma located near the brainstem
(figure~\ref{fig:tumour}).

The fit to the $\beta^+$ activity allows the determination of the distal edge of
the proton beam ($R$), which is the critical measurement to be pinned down in
proton beam treatment~\cite{zhu2013}. The goal is to minimise the {\em bias} of the
$R$ determination (that is, the difference between $R$ and the true distal edge
$D$), as well as $\sigma_R$ (the r.m.s.\ spread of $R$ over an ensemble of
acquisitions).

The input to CBS is the list of lines of response (LORs) produced by a PET scanner
specified by a number of parameters, including PET diameter, axial field of view
(AFOV), crystal dimensions and crystal type. For each scanner a sensitivity image
is computed, and MLEM~\cite{shepp_vardi} is used to reconstruct the activity image
$A$. $R$ is estimated through a depth-profile extraction and an endpoint
estimation, as described below.

\section{The scanner}

The reference scanner is a CRYSP\footnote{CRYogenic Sensitive PET} model, with an
inner radius $R_{\mathrm{inner}} = \SI{387}{\milli\metre}$ and an AFOV of
\SI{1024}{\milli\metre}. It deploys monolithic BGO crystals of
$50 \times 50$~mm$^2$ surface and a depth of 3.3 radiation lengths
(3.3~X$_0$), or \SI{37}{\milli\metre}. The scanner has 20 wheels and 48 crystals
per wheel, for a total of 960 crystals. The energy resolution of the scanner is
10\% at \SI{511}{\kilo\electronvolt} and the spatial resolution in the three
coordinates is \SI{3.5}{\milli\metre} FWHM. The coincidence time window for the
LORs is \SI{3}{\nano\second}~\cite{soleti_crysp}.

\section{Depth-profile extraction and endpoint estimation}
\label{sec:depthprofile}

Let \(\lambda(\mathbf{r})\) denote the reconstructed activity image, resampled
onto a beam-aligned grid with \(\mathbf{r}=(x,y,z)\) and the beam (depth) along
\(z\). The one-dimensional depth--activity profile is the transverse integral
over the full image plane,
\begin{equation}
  P(z) \;=\; \int \lambda(x,y,z)\,\mathrm{d}x\,\mathrm{d}y
        \;\approx\; \Delta x\,\Delta y \sum_{i,j}
        \lambda_{ijk(z)},
  \label{eq:profile}
\end{equation}
carried over the whole transverse extent of the reconstruction grid.
Integrating the full plane makes \(P(z)\) the same observable for the
reconstructed image and for the generated activity, and keeps the endpoint
independent of the transverse beam envelope: the halo produced by multiple
Coulomb scattering widens with depth, so a fixed transverse aperture captures
a depth-decreasing fraction of the activity and displaces the fitted endpoint
proximally (by \SIrange{2}{3}{\milli\metre} for a disc of
\SI{13}{\milli\metre} radius in the geometry of this study).
Equation~\eqref{eq:profile} is an integration
(a sum), never an average or a normalisation: summing preserves the counting
statistics on which the endpoint fit and \(\sigma_R\) depend. Because
\(P(z)\) is a sum of voxel counts it is, to first approximation, Poisson with
variance \(\operatorname{Var}[P(z)]\approx\bar{P}(z)\); the MLEM reconstruction
correlates neighbouring voxels, so this is a working approximation used only to
weight the fit, while the authoritative endpoint uncertainty is the ensemble
spread defined below.

The activity profile is not a single sigmoid---it rises from the entrance,
plateaus, and falls at the distal edge, which lies \emph{upstream} of the Bragg
peak because the \(\beta^{+}\)-production channels close below their reaction
thresholds. The endpoint is therefore estimated within a \emph{fixed} distal
analysis window \(\mathcal{W}=[z_0^{\mathrm{nom}}-\delta_-,\,
z_0^{\mathrm{nom}}+\delta_+]\), bracketing the falloff and defined from the
\emph{nominal} range, identically for every arm and realisation; a
per-realisation peak search would correlate \(\mathcal{W}\) with the very
fluctuations being measured. Inside \(\mathcal{W}\) the falloff is modelled by a
complementary error function,
\begin{equation}
  \hat{P}(z) \;=\; b \;+\; a\,\tfrac{1}{2}
    \operatorname{erfc}\!\left(\frac{z-z_0}{\sqrt{2}\,w}\right),
  \label{eq:erfc}
\end{equation}
so that \(z_0\) is by construction the \(R_{50}\) point --- the same activity-edge
observable, fitted on a whole-plane profile, that Zapien-Campos
\emph{et al.}~\cite{zapien2025} adopt as the PET-activity range
parameter --- \(a\) and \(b\) absorb
the plateau height and background (making \(z_0\) invariant to the absolute
activity scale), and \(w\) is the edge width. Any falloff level follows
analytically,
\begin{equation}
  R_x \;=\; z_0 \;+\; \sqrt{2}\,w\,\operatorname{erfc}^{-1}(2x),
  \label{eq:Rx}
\end{equation}
with \(R_{50}\) the most precisely determined point (the steepest slope of the
edge) and \(R_{80},R_{20}\) as tail diagnostics. The parameters are obtained by
minimising the Poisson-weighted residual
\begin{equation}
  \chi^{2} \;=\; \sum_{z\in\mathcal{W}}
    \frac{\bigl[P(z)-\hat{P}(z)\bigr]^{2}}{\max\!\bigl(P(z),1\bigr)},
  \label{eq:chi2}
\end{equation}
which yields the parameters \(z_0^{(k)}, w^{(k)}\) and their covariance for
each realisation \(k\).

The predictor of the beam endpoint is the \emph{practical endpoint}, obtained
by extrapolating the tangent of the fitted edge at \(z_0\) down to the
baseline,
\begin{equation}
  R_p \;=\; z_0 \;+\; \sqrt{\tfrac{\pi}{2}}\,w,
  \label{eq:Rp}
\end{equation}
the construction used for the practical range of electron beams; on the fitted
edge it marks the point where the signal has fallen to \(10.5\%\) of the
plateau, and its uncertainty follows from the \((z_0,w)\) covariance. The
extrapolated crossing of the activity towards zero is reported at a fixed
small fraction of the plateau through Eq.~\eqref{eq:Rx}
(\(R_{1\%}=z_0+2.326\,w\)); it lies distal of \(R_p\), and since it sits far
from the steep part of the edge its uncertainty is dominated by that of the
width \(w\). \(R_p\) therefore serves as the primary predictor and
\(R_{1\%}\) as a tail diagnostic. Both constructions, applied identically to
the depth--dose curve, give the endpoint-to-endpoint distance between the
\(\beta^{+}\) activity and the delivered dose.

Finally, over the ensemble of \(K\) realisations at a given delivered dose, the
mean endpoint estimates the systematic activity-edge position while its spread is
the terminal figure of merit,
\begin{equation}
  \bar{R}_p = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k} R_p^{(k)}, \qquad
  \sigma_R = \sqrt{\frac{1}{K-1}\sum_{k}\bigl(R_p^{(k)}-\bar{R}_p\bigr)^{2}}
           \;\sim\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}},
  \label{eq:sigmaR}
\end{equation}
recovering the counts-limited scaling in the detected-coincidence number \(N\).
The methodological invariant of the comparison is that the grid, the window
\(\mathcal{W}\), the model \eqref{eq:erfc} and the estimator \eqref{eq:chi2}
are identical across the three
geometries: the \emph{only} quantity that differs entering \(\sigma_R\) is the
reconstructed image content. This is also where the open dual-head geometry is
tested most sharply---its limited-angle artefact elongates the activity
distribution, and whenever that elongation projects onto the beam ($z$) axis it
biases \(R_p\) directly, so \(\sigma_R\) is reported as a function of beam-axis
orientation relative to the head gap.
