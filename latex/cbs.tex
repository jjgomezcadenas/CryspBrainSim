\begin{abstract}
Proton therapy concentrates dose in the sharp Bragg peak characteristic of hadron ionisation, sparing the tissue beyond it. While this sharpness is the technology's advantage, it demands careful planning and monitoring of the beam range, in particular the distal edge (the point where the beam stops). A range error of a few millimetres can underdose the tumour or overexpose a critical organ behind it, so range verification is a central problem of the field. A viable {\em in vivo} technique  is to image the positron emitters, mostly $^{15}$O and $^{11}$C, produced along the beam path, inside the treatment room, either immediately or shortly after the irradiation,  before the short-lived $^{15}$O has decayed. Open PET geometries allow in-beam, measurement with essentially zero delay after irradiation. On the other hand, a dedicated, optimised, in-room PET can provide a full ring, high AFOV, high-sensitivity measurement, in exchange of a time delay which can be as short as 1--2 minutes. 

In this paper we study the potential of CRYSP (CRYogenic Sensitive PET). The base of the technology is the use of large monolithic crystals operating at cryogenic (liquid nitrogen) temperatures. We show that a large (1 m AFOV) in-room scanner applying this technology allows a precise distal edge determination at low proton doses, and is thus suitable as a precise verification instrument in proton therapy. Importantly, long AFOV CRYSP scanners allow verification of proton treatment in any region of the body (as opposed to specialised head-scanners, for example) and can be built as dedicated devices at an affordable cost.

Monolithic CsI provides high sensitivity at low cost, resolves the depth of interaction, removing parallax, and reaches an energy resolution that rejects the scattered background through a narrow window around 511 keV. Because the measurement is made at low rate after the beam, the detector is free of pile-up, and the crystal can be optimised for energy resolution regardless of decay time.

\section{Introduction}

CryspBrainSim (CBS) fits the activity distribution of the $\beta^+$ emitters
produced in the decay of the various isotopes activated by a proton beam crossing
(brain) tissue and delivering a conformal dose into a (head) tumour. The case
chosen as representative is an ependymoma located near the brainstem
(figure~\ref{fig:tumour}).

\begin{figure}[htb]
  \centering

  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figs/ependymoma_mri.png}
  \caption{Gadolinium-enhanced T1-weighted MRI of a fourth-ventricle ependymoma.
  \emph{(Left)} coronal and \emph{(right)} sagittal views show a large midline
  enhancing mass invading the roof of the fourth ventricle and growing downward
  toward the foramen magnum, with the brainstem displaced anteriorly. The
  tumour--brainstem interface visible here is precisely the region where the
  distal edge of a proton beam must be placed. Image credit: https://case.edu/med/neurology/NR/cerebelepend2.htm.}
  \label{fig:tumour}
\end{figure}

The fit to the $\beta^+$ activity allows the determination of the distal edge of
the proton beam ($R$), which is the critical measurement to be pinned down in
proton beam treatment~\cite{zhu2013}. The goal is to minimise the {\em bias} of the
$R$ determination (that is, the difference between $R$ and the true distal edge
$D$), as well as $\sigma_R$ (the r.m.s.\ spread of $R$ over an ensemble of
acquisitions).

The input to CBS is the list of lines of response (LORs) produced by a PET scanner
specified by a number of parameters, including PET diameter, axial field of view
(AFOV), crystal dimensions and crystal type. For each scanner a sensitivity image
is computed, and MLEM~\cite{shepp_vardi} is used to reconstruct the activity image
$A$. $R$ is estimated through a depth-profile extraction and an endpoint
estimation, as described below.

\section{The scanner}

The reference scanner is a CRYSP\footnote{CRYogenic Sensitive PET} model, with an
inner radius $R_{\mathrm{inner}} = \SI{387}{\milli\metre}$ and an AFOV of
\SI{1024}{\milli\metre}. It deploys monolithic BGO crystals of
$50 \times 50$~mm$^2$ surface and a depth of 3.3 radiation lengths
(3.3~X$_0$), or \SI{37}{\milli\metre}. The scanner has 20 wheels and 48 crystals
per wheel, for a total of 960 crystals. The energy resolution of the scanner is
10\% at \SI{511}{\kilo\electronvolt} and the spatial resolution in the three
coordinates is \SI{3.5}{\milli\metre} FWHM. The coincidence time window for the
LORs is \SI{3}{\nano\second}~\cite{soleti_crysp}.

\section{Depth-profile extraction and endpoint estimation}
\label{sec:depthprofile}

Let \(\lambda(\mathbf{r})\) denote the reconstructed activity image, resampled
onto a beam-aligned grid with \(\mathbf{r}=(x,y,z)\) and the beam (depth) along
\(z\). The one-dimensional depth--activity profile is the transverse integral
over the full image plane,
\begin{equation}
  P(z) \;=\; \int \lambda(x,y,z)\,\mathrm{d}x\,\mathrm{d}y
        \;\approx\; \Delta x\,\Delta y \sum_{i,j}
        \lambda_{ijk(z)},
  \label{eq:profile}
\end{equation}
carried over the whole transverse extent of the reconstruction grid.
Integrating the full plane makes \(P(z)\) the same observable for the
reconstructed image and for the generated activity, and keeps the endpoint
independent of the transverse beam envelope: the halo produced by multiple
Coulomb scattering widens with depth, so a fixed transverse aperture captures
a depth-decreasing fraction of the activity and displaces the fitted endpoint
proximally (by \SIrange{2}{3}{\milli\metre} for a disc of
\SI{13}{\milli\metre} radius in the geometry of this study).
Equation~\eqref{eq:profile} is an integration
(a sum), never an average or a normalisation: summing preserves the counting
statistics on which the endpoint fit and \(\sigma_R\) depend. Because
\(P(z)\) is a sum of voxel counts it is, to first approximation, Poisson with
variance \(\operatorname{Var}[P(z)]\approx\bar{P}(z)\); the MLEM reconstruction
correlates neighbouring voxels, so this is a working approximation used only to
weight the fit, while the authoritative endpoint uncertainty is the ensemble
spread defined below.

The activity profile is not a single sigmoid---it rises from the entrance,
plateaus, and falls at the distal edge, which lies \emph{upstream} of the Bragg
peak because the \(\beta^{+}\)-production channels close below their reaction
thresholds. The endpoint is therefore estimated within a \emph{fixed} distal
analysis window \(\mathcal{W}=[z_0^{\mathrm{nom}}-\delta_-,\,
z_0^{\mathrm{nom}}+\delta_+]\), bracketing the falloff and defined from the
\emph{nominal} range, identically for every arm and realisation; a
per-realisation peak search would correlate \(\mathcal{W}\) with the very
fluctuations being measured. Inside \(\mathcal{W}\) the falloff is modelled by a
complementary error function,
\begin{equation}
  \hat{P}(z) \;=\; b \;+\; a\,\tfrac{1}{2}
    \operatorname{erfc}\!\left(\frac{z-z_0}{\sqrt{2}\,w}\right),
  \label{eq:erfc}
\end{equation}
so that \(z_0\) is by construction the \(R_{50}\) point --- the same activity-edge
observable, fitted on a whole-plane profile, that Zapien-Campos
\emph{et al.}~\cite{zapien2025} adopt as the PET-activity range
parameter --- \(a\) and \(b\) absorb
the plateau height and background (making \(z_0\) invariant to the absolute
activity scale), and \(w\) is the edge width. Any falloff level follows
analytically,
\begin{equation}
  R_x \;=\; z_0 \;+\; \sqrt{2}\,w\,\operatorname{erfc}^{-1}(2x),
  \label{eq:Rx}
\end{equation}
with \(R_{50}\) the most precisely determined point (the steepest slope of the
edge) and \(R_{80},R_{20}\) as tail diagnostics. The parameters are obtained by
minimising the Poisson-weighted residual
\begin{equation}
  \chi^{2} \;=\; \sum_{z\in\mathcal{W}}
    \frac{\bigl[P(z)-\hat{P}(z)\bigr]^{2}}{\max\!\bigl(P(z),1\bigr)},
  \label{eq:chi2}
\end{equation}
which yields the parameters \(z_0^{(k)}, w^{(k)}\) and their covariance for
each realisation \(k\).

The predictor of the beam endpoint is the \emph{practical endpoint}, obtained
by extrapolating the tangent of the fitted edge at \(z_0\) down to the
baseline,
\begin{equation}
  R_p \;=\; z_0 \;+\; \sqrt{\tfrac{\pi}{2}}\,w,
  \label{eq:Rp}
\end{equation}
the construction used for the practical range of electron beams; on the fitted
edge it marks the point where the signal has fallen to \(10.5\%\) of the
plateau, and its uncertainty follows from the \((z_0,w)\) covariance. The
extrapolated crossing of the activity towards zero is reported at a fixed
small fraction of the plateau through Eq.~\eqref{eq:Rx}
(\(R_{1\%}=z_0+2.326\,w\)); it lies distal of \(R_p\), and since it sits far
from the steep part of the edge its uncertainty is dominated by that of the
width \(w\). \(R_p\) therefore serves as the primary predictor and
\(R_{1\%}\) as a tail diagnostic. Both constructions, applied identically to
the depth--dose curve, give the endpoint-to-endpoint distance between the
\(\beta^{+}\) activity and the delivered dose.

Finally, over the ensemble of \(K\) realisations at a given delivered dose, the
mean endpoint estimates the systematic activity-edge position while its spread is
the terminal figure of merit,
\begin{equation}
  \bar{R}_p = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k} R_p^{(k)}, \qquad
  \sigma_R = \sqrt{\frac{1}{K-1}\sum_{k}\bigl(R_p^{(k)}-\bar{R}_p\bigr)^{2}}
           \;\sim\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}},
  \label{eq:sigmaR}
\end{equation}
recovering the counts-limited scaling in the detected-coincidence number \(N\).
The methodological invariant of the comparison is that the grid, the window
\(\mathcal{W}\), the model \eqref{eq:erfc} and the estimator \eqref{eq:chi2}
are identical across the three
geometries: the \emph{only} quantity that differs entering \(\sigma_R\) is the
reconstructed image content. This is also where the open dual-head geometry is
tested most sharply---its limited-angle artefact elongates the activity
distribution, and whenever that elongation projects onto the beam ($z$) axis it
biases \(R_p\) directly, so \(\sigma_R\) is reported as a function of beam-axis
orientation relative to the head gap.
