\begin{abstract}
Proton beam therapy (PBT) concentrates dose in the sharp Bragg peak characteristic of hadron ionisation, sparing the tissue beyond it. While this sharpness is the technology's advantage, it demands careful planning and monitoring of the beam range, and in particular of the distal edge (the point where the beam stops beyond the tumour), since an error of a few millimetres in its estimated position can underdose the tumour or overexpose a critical organ behind it. A viable \emph{in vivo} technique is to image the positron emitters, mostly $^{15}$O and $^{11}$C, produced along the beam path, inside the treatment room, either immediately or shortly after the irradiation. Open PET geometries allow in-beam measurement with essentially zero delay after irradiation. However, in the configurations studied, the loss of activity during a delay of a few minutes is offset by the high sensitivity and good energy and spatial resolution of an in-room full-ring scanner.

Here, we propose the use of in-room PET scanners based on the CRYSP concept. The acronym, {\em CRYogenic Sensitive PET}, refers to the key innovation of these devices: suitable scintillators such as CsI and BGO dramatically increase their light yield at cryogenic temperatures. To quantify their performance we define a scenario that models the treatment of a head tumour. We apply a typical treatment dose, with a spread-out Bragg peak (SOBP) designed to deliver conformal coverage of the tumour. The resulting $\beta^+$ activity is then recorded by our CRYSP scanners. An erfc fit is performed on the image projection, from which the depth of the $50\%$ point (half-height) on the distal fall-off of the
activity, \Rc, is obtained. Next, the RMS variation of \Rc, $\sigma_R$, is quantified. We study the impact of the physics (defined by isotope decay and washout) and the scanner response (defined by the apparatus sensitivity and its spatial and energy resolutions). We consider CsI and BGO crystals, and several scanner configurations. In all cases studied, BGO yields the smaller statistical uncertainty, while CsI combines competitive precision with lower cost and greater material availability.
  

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Proton beam therapy (PBT)
exploits the favourable interaction properties of protons in matter, in
particular their finite range and the characteristic dose maximum in depth
known as the Bragg peak~\cite{Jaekel2020}. Thanks to the steep dose gradients
and the sharp distal fall-off, proton beams can achieve highly conformal
tumour coverage while better sparing the surrounding normal tissue in
comparison to conventional photon radiation~\cite{Jaekel2020}. Full
exploitation of these physical advantages in clinical practice, however,
requires accurate knowledge of the beam stopping position in the patient, the so-called distal edge.

The depth at which a proton beam stops---and therefore the position of the
distal dose edge---is determined by the stopping power of the tissue it
traverses. This stopping power, in turn,  is 
inferred from X-ray CT images through a semi-empirical calibration that maps the X-ray attenuation recorded
in each voxel onto stopping-power ratios relative to water~\cite{Paganetti2012}. 
Because this calibration is only approximate, it
introduces an intrinsic range uncertainty of up to about $3\%$, which
propagates directly into the predicted location of the distal edge; further
uncertainty arises from day-to-day variations in patient anatomy and
positioning~\cite{Paganetti2012}.  


\emph{In vivo} verification of the delivered range gives a direct check on the
stopping position and a means of tightening the margins that presently absorb
the range uncertainty~\cite{Paganetti2012}. Among the techniques investigated for \emph{in vivo} verification of the beam range,
positron-emission tomography (PET) is one of the most widely studied. When the
beam energy is above the threshold for nuclear interactions between the
incoming protons and the tissue nuclei, positron-emitting nuclei (e.g.
$^{11}$C, $^{15}$O) are produced along the beam path, and the back-to-back
$511\,\mathrm{keV}$ photons ensuing from positron annihilation can be detected
in coincidence, providing non-invasive information on the beam stopping
position~\cite{Bennett1978,Enghardt2004}. The irradiation-induced activity
exhibits a pronounced fall-off shortly before the Bragg peak, whose
correlation with the delivered dose can be exploited for range
monitoring~\cite{Parodi2018}. 


Because positron emitters are produced only where the protons are still above
the threshold for nuclear reactions, the $\beta^{+}$ activity profile falls off
slightly upstream of the dose Bragg peak.
%, as shown in
%Fig.~\ref{fig:dose_activity}. 
The range, however, can be recovered by relating the
activity edge to the dose edge.


The dominant emitters in the $\beta^{+}$ trail that follows the proton beam are
$^{15}$O (half-life $2$~min) and $^{11}$C ($20$~min). Their different lifetimes
set the time evolution of the signal: $^{15}$O
dominates the first minutes after irradiation and then decays quickly, while
$^{11}$C carries the activity at later times. The acquisition window therefore
fixes both the number of detected counts and the isotope composition.


PET verification has been realised in three acquisition geometries, set apart by
when and where the signal is collected~\cite{Shakirin2011,Kraan2015}. 

In
\emph{offline} imaging the patient is taken to a nearby PET/CT some
$15$--$30$~minutes after treatment, which gives the full angular coverage of a
closed ring. The delay, however, carries serious costs: (i) reduced sensitivity,
since the abundant $^{15}$O has all but vanished ten minutes after the
irradiation; (ii) significant, and hard to model, biological washout; and (iii) a
co-registration step, needed because the patient is set up afresh on a separate
scanner, so the measured activity and the treatment plan lie in different
coordinate frames. Co-registration matches the PET/CT to the planning CT, and the
residual misalignment, typically a few millimetres, maps directly into an
apparent shift of the range~\cite{Knopf2009,zhu2013}.

In \emph{in-beam} imaging the signal is collected
during and immediately after irradiation with the detectors at the treatment
position, which retains the short-lived isotopes and keeps the patient
stationary. In order to leave the beam path clear, in-beam PET has typically used two planar detector heads~\cite{Crespo2006,Ferrero2018}. While in-beam minimises the effect of radioactive decay and washout, and eliminates the need for co-registration, the price to pay is reduced angular coverage, which dramatically lowers sensitivity and introduces limited-angle image
artefacts that grow away from the field centre~\cite{Crespo2006,Moglioni2022}. It also clutters the beam-delivery zone. 

 In \emph{in-room} imaging a dedicated
scanner stands beside the beam and the patient is imaged on the treatment couch
after a transfer time which can be minimised to about two minutes. The short delay minimises the effects of both radioactive decay and washout, while the full ring geometry eliminates artefacts, and, importantly, allows a short scan, minimising in-scan washout and patient movement. Since
the patient stays on the couch, the couch motion itself---a known rigid transform
read from its positioning system---aligns the plan and PET coordinate frames, so
the registration follows from this couch transform~\cite{Zhu2011,Min2013}.
Furthermore, the scanner sits at some distance from the beam, so it can be
operated in parallel with beam delivery, potentially improving patient
throughput.

The systematic limitations on the precision of distal-point determination using $\beta^+$ activity depend
on many factors in addition to the scanner and the acquisition mode (offline,
in-beam, in-room). They include the reaction cross
sections~\cite{Espana2011}, the modelling of biological
washout~\cite{Mizuno2003,parodi2008}, the type of tissue being imaged, and the
body region being treated~\cite{Knopf2009}. Results on head-and-neck sites are
promising~\cite{Knopf2009,Min2013}, while patient motion has so far limited the
precision reachable in lung tumours~\cite{Knopf2009}. The best estimator of the
distal edge is likewise still under study, and several approaches have been
tried~\cite{grogg2013}. A sigmoid/erfc fit that locates the \Rc\ point of the
activity image and relates it to the \Roc\ point of the dose\footnote{\Roc\ is the depth of the $80\%$ point on the
distal fall-off of the \emph{dose}, the standard clinical proxy for the beam
range, due to its robustness to range straggling; \Rc\ is the depth of the $50\%$ point (half-height) on the distal fall-off of the
\emph{activity}, its steepest and hence best-determined point. The two are
separated by an offset set by the beam and tissue model, whose determination is critical for the technique.
} is statistically
robust, but relating it to \Roc\ requires a well-tuned Monte Carlo description of the ion
beam~\cite{Espana2011}. This has motivated attempts to fit the distal edge
directly from the data, which carry their own systematics~\cite{grogg2013}.
Overall the problem remains open, and the heterogeneity of the systems used by
the different groups complicates the picture. Among the options, offline PET
imaging is disfavoured, since it amplifies {\em avoidable} effects such as
reduced ion activity and increased washout, and in-beam PET has so far been
confined to research systems rather than clinical use.

A high-sensitivity in-room PET scanner can address several limitations of existing range-verification systems. Rapid and reproducible transfer of the treatment couch from the beam position to the scanner can be incorporated into the facility design and clinical workflow. The combination of high sensitivity with good spatial and energy resolution improves the statistical quality of the activity image, while stable scanner parameters facilitate accurate simulation of the detector response. High sensitivity also allows for a short scan, minimising the effects of washout and patient movement. Together, these features provide favourable conditions for developing a distal-edge verification method for clinical use.

Here, we propose an in-room PET scanner based on the CRYSP concept \cite{soleti_crysp}. The acronym, {\em CRYogenic Sensitive PET}, refers to the key innovation of these devices: suitable scintillators such as CsI and BGO dramatically increase their light yield at cryogenic temperatures, in exchange for a slower decay constant. 

To quantify the application of CRYSP scanners to PBT, we define a scenario that idealises one of the more significant applications of PBT: the treatment of a head tumour, specifically an ependymoma (Fig.~\ref{fig:tumour}). We define a phantom modelling a patient's head and consisting of a uniform brain-tissue ellipsoid, and apply a typical treatment dose, with a SOBP beam designed to deliver conformal coverage of the tumour. The resulting $\beta^+$ activity is then recorded by our CRYSP scanners. An erfc fit is performed on the image projection, from which the \Rc\ edge is obtained.

Our objective here is to quantify the RMS variation of \Rc\ ($\sigma_R$) due to known factors. These include: a) the impact of the PET itself (sensitivity, AFOV, resolution); b) the impact of radioactive decay, for different start-of-scan and total-scan times; and c) the impact of washout. Effects (a) and (b) together set the ``no-washout'' $\sigma_R$ reported below, while (c) adds the washout cost.

Notice that we do not attempt to quantify systematic uncertainties such as density variations, the fidelity of Monte Carlo modelling (cross sections in particular), and imperfect knowledge of the washout model. Previous studies estimate that these effects contribute range uncertainties of a few millimetres, even under favourable conditions~\cite{Espana2011,Knopf2009,Mizuno2003,Paganetti2012}. Instead, our goal is to determine whether the statistical contribution of the CRYSP scanner, $\sigma_R$, is small compared with these systematic uncertainties. If so, the scanner could be used to investigate these uncertainties under controlled experimental conditions and, eventually, for clinical range verification.

\section{CRYSP scanners}
\label{sec:crysp}
Pure CsI is a fast but faint scintillator at room temperature,
with a light yield of about $5\,000$ photons/MeV and a decay constant of
$\sim\!15$~ns. Upon cooling to cryogenic temperatures ($\sim\!77$~K) its light
yield increases by a factor of about 20, reaching roughly
$1\times10^{5}$~photons/MeV, while the decay constant lengthens by a factor of
about 50 to $\sim\!800$~ns~\cite{soleti_crysp}. A similar behaviour is found
in BGO: recent measurements with a SiPM-coupled crystal yield an intrinsic
light output of about $1.4\times10^{4}$~photons/MeV at dry-ice temperature
($195$~K) and about $2.9\times10^{4}$~photons/MeV at liquid-nitrogen
temperature ($77$~K), to be compared with $\sim\!9\times10^{3}$~photons/MeV at
room temperature, again at the expense of a markedly slower response, with the
decay constant growing from $\sim\!300$~ns at $300$~K to about $1.8$~$\mu$s at
$195$~K and to a two-component decay of $\sim\!3$--$9$~$\mu$s at
$77$~K~\cite{ding_bgo}. The relevant figures are summarised in
Table~\ref{tab:cryo_scint}.

 In both cases, the intense light emission compensates the relatively slow decay constant, allowing a coincidence time resolution (CTR) of the order of a few nanoseconds. The long integration time needed to measure the signal, however, has a real impact on conventional PET imaging. As discussed in~\cite{soleti_crysp}, the impact of pile-up in a CsI scanner is modest and can be further reduced with dedicated electronics. This may also be the case for a BGO scanner operating at 195~K, but pile-up in the case of BGO at 77~K is too high for typical high-activity medical imaging. However, the activity in PBT scans, even at large doses, is two to three orders of magnitude smaller than that of imaging applications (Figure~\ref{fig:activity_iso}). This, combined with the delay time ($\sim 2$ minutes) until the start of the acquisition, results in no significant pile-up for CsI or BGO at 195~K and only a very small effect ($\sim 1\%$) for BGO at 77~K. Thus, the relatively slow scintillation response of these devices---the main limitation for clinical PET imaging---becomes a non-issue for PBT.
 
 In exchange, the large light yield has several benefits. First, it brings the energy resolution of the scanners essentially to the Fano limit. In CsI, a resolution of 6\% has been measured~\cite{soleti_csi}, and BGO can likely reach a similar value with enough light yield, though we adopt a conservative $10\%$ for it in this study. Improved energy resolution reduces the contribution of scattered coincidences to the reconstructed image and thereby limits one source of image degradation. Second, the use of monolithic crystals read out by SiPMs, with reconstruction of the three coordinates of the incident gamma ray, becomes feasible. The high light yield provides the photon statistics required for good energy resolution and effective light sharing among large SiPMs---typically $6 \times 6$~mm$^2$ in the proposed design~\cite{soleti_crysp}---while convolutional neural networks achieve uniform spatial resolution in all three coordinates, avoiding the parallax degradation characteristic of pixelated crystals. Uniform three-dimensional spatial resolution and the absence of parallax degradation reduce position-dependent blurring in the reconstructed image.

 The scanners also offer a very competitive cost. CsI is the cheapest option and is readily available. BGO, on the other hand, although a common material for PET with a very competitive cost when compared with LYSO, has the drawbacks of restricted supply---an important fraction of production comes from China---and greater cost volatility. In both cases, monolithic crystals reduce costs relative to their pixelated analogues. Furthermore, the readout is considerably cheaper. In large quantities the cost of the SiPMs is proportional to the deployed surface and independent of pixel size. The number of electronics channels, by contrast, does depend on it. CRYSP crystals are read out by matrices of $8 \times 8$ SiPMs of $6 \times 6$~mm$^2$, while conventional scanners typically use $3 \times 3$~mm$^2$ pixels. Thus the number of channels in a conventional scanner is approximately four times as large as that in a CRYSP scanner. Finally, there is the cost of the electronics channels themselves. LYSO scanners use fast electronics and synchronisation between system clocks in the range of hundreds of picoseconds (to take advantage of TOF capabilities), while CRYSP scanners need to handle large signals with synchronisation at the nanosecond level. Typical costs of TOF electronics for LYSO scanners are around 20~\euro/channel, while the estimated cost for CRYSP electronics is of the order of 12~\euro/channel. The unit costs of the individual detector elements are collected in Table~\ref{tab:element-costs}, and Figure~\ref{fig:cost} summarises the resulting scanner cost.
 
An important factor not fully captured by the price tag is that of material availability. LYSO relies on lutetium, a rare earth, and BGO on germanium and bismuth; all are strategically controlled materials whose supply is concentrated in a few actors, China in particular. This introduces uncertainties in supply (and variability in cost) that are absent in the case of CsI, a common and readily available material. 

Importantly for this application, monolithic crystals of CsI(Tl) are also readily available and offer performance similar to that of cryogenic pure CsI in both light yield (50,000 photons/MeV, a factor of two less than pure CsI at 77~K but still sufficient for good energy and spatial resolution) and scintillation decay time (also of the order of 1~$\mu$s). Like cryogenic BGO, the use of CsI(Tl) is problematic in high-activity applications because of its afterglow, but this effect is irrelevant in the low-activity environment of PBT.

Our setup is chosen to model one of the most relevant applications of PBT. Ependymomas are rare tumours of the central nervous system which arise from the ependymal cells lining the cerebral ventricles and
the central canal of the spinal cord~\citep{donahue2022,macdonald2013}. In children they are the third most common neuroepithelial tumour,
comprising roughly \SIrange{5}{10}{\percent} of childhood brain tumours, or about \num{3.4} cases per million children in Europe, aged \numrange{0}{14} years~\citep{peris2006},  with a median age at diagnosis
of only \SIrange{4}{6}{\years}. In children, ependymomas grow in the lower-back part of the skull, specifically in the cavity that holds the brainstem and cerebellum, and tend to be very aggressive (Figure~\ref{fig:tumour}). The beam must therefore be adjusted to cover the tumour fully while sparing healthy cerebellar tissue. This general requirement is particularly important in children and translates into the need to predict the distal edge with great accuracy. A target value often quoted in the literature is $\sim 1$~mm. 

At the same time, ependymomas in adults appear predominantly as a spinal-cord tumour, with about \SI{64}{\percent} arising in the spine~\citep{mcguire2009}. 


\subsection{The scanner}
The wide range of tumours that can be treated with a proton beam drives the CRYSP configurations that we have selected for this study, described below. 

We take as a reference a ``total body PET'' (TBP) with an
inner radius $R_{\mathrm{inner}} = \SI{387}{\milli\metre}$ and an axial length of
\SI{1024}{\milli\metre}, following the model presented in \cite{soleti_crysp}. The scanner deploys monolithic CsI or BGO crystals of $48 \times 48$~mm$^2$ surface and a depth of 2 radiation lengths
(2~X$_0$). It has 20 wheels and 48 crystals
per wheel, for a total of 960 crystals. The crystals and the SiPMs operate inside a cryostat. Signals are extracted through flat kapton cables through suitable feedthroughs and read with custom electronics. The operation temperature is  \SI{77}{\kelvin}
 (liquid nitrogen), with two important qualifications. It is possible to operate a CsI(Tl) scanner at room temperature, and BGO can be operated at \SI{195}{\kelvin} instead of \SI{77}{\kelvin}, trading a factor of two in light yield for a much shorter scintillation decay time. 
 
 In addition to the reference scanner, we consider two more configurations per crystal: a ``large AFOV'' (LAFOV) configuration with an axial length of \SI{500}{\milli\metre} (same diameter as the reference scanner), and a ``compact AFOV'' (CAFOV) configuration with an axial length of \SI{350}{\milli\metre}. The energy resolution of the scanners at \SI{511}{\kilo\electronvolt} is 6\% for CsI~\cite{soleti_crysp,soleti_csi} and
10\% for BGO---a conservative value given the high light yields, but still two times better than the results often quoted in the literature. The spatial resolution in the three
coordinates is \SI{3.5}{\milli\metre} FWHM~\cite{soleti_crysp}.


If the only aim were to verify proton treatments of tumours of the brain and
skull base, a compact configuration would be sufficient: the
target always sits in the same place, and a short axial field of view (AFOV)
covers it comfortably. The case for a larger scanner, on the other hand,  is its flexibility, e.g., the ability to deploy a single
instrument across the range of anatomical sites that proton therapy treats.
The CAFOV configuration is adequate for the great majority of
indications. Head and skull-base targets, and the localised deep-trunk targets
(prostate, pancreas, liver, kidney), are each smaller than \SI{35}{\centi\metre}
and are imaged by centring the region of interest in the scanner. The LAFOV (or even the TBP), on the other hand,
may be ideally suited for craniospinal irradiation spanning the whole neuraxis over roughly \SIrange{60}{70}{\centi\metre}. The longer axial field of view also increases geometrical acceptance and hence scanner sensitivity. The tradeoff is the cost, illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:cost}.

\subsection{Simulations}
Each simulated study corresponds to a dose of \SI{1}{\gray}. The parameters of the scan are $t_d$ (the delay, relative to the start of the dose, at which the scan starts) and $t_s$ (the scan duration). Each scan records
the list of lines of response (LORs) produced by a PET scanner. 
MLEM~\cite{shepp_vardi} is used to reconstruct the activity image
$A(x,y,z)$. \Rc\ is estimated through a depth-profile extraction, followed by an erfc fit.  

To find $\sigma_R$, ten independent \SI{1}{\gray} Monte Carlo samples are run and pooled into a single event sample; the thinning method (Sec.~\ref{sec:thinning}) is then executed over this pool to estimate $\sigma_R$.

The calibration offset $\DR = \Roc - \Rc$ between the activity midpoint \Rc\ and
the dose edge \Roc\ is fixed for a given setup and is determined beforehand (for
example, via a precise simulation of the setup); on real data one then fits \Rc\
and adds \DR\ to recover the distal edge \Roc\ (Sec.~\ref{sec:estimator}).

\section{The estimator}
\label{sec:estimator}


\begin{figure}[htbp!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/recon_projections_bgo.png}
  \caption{Orthogonal projections of the reconstructed activity of one
    \SI{1}{\gray} BGO run (transaxial, coronal, sagittal). The dashed curve
    is the phantom outline; the dotted line marks the fitted \Rc.}
  \label{fig:projections}
\end{figure}


Figure~\ref{fig:projections} shows orthogonal projections of one reconstructed
run. The depth profile is the activity integrated over the full transverse
plane at each depth,
%
\begin{equation}
  P(z) \;=\; \iint A(x,y,z)\,\mathrm{d}x\,\mathrm{d}y ,
  \label{eq:profile}
\end{equation}
%
which makes the profile insensitive to the transverse spreading of the beam
with depth. The distal fall-off is fitted with a complementary error
function plus a baseline,
%
\begin{equation}
  P(z) \;=\; b + \frac{a}{2}\,
  \operatorname{erfc}\!\left(\frac{z - z_0}{\sqrt{2}\,w}\right),
  \label{eq:erfc}
\end{equation}
%
inside a fixed window bracketing the edge ($\SI{-36.5}{mm} \le z \le \SI{-1.5}{mm}$
for the nominal beam), with Poisson weights. The four parameters are the
baseline $b$, the plateau amplitude $a$, the midpoint $z_0$ and the edge
width $w$. From this definition it follows that $\Rc \equiv z_0$. 
Figure~\ref{fig:fits} shows the fit on one run of each reference scanner.

\begin{figure}[htbp!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{figs/fit_all_events_bgo.png}
  \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{figs/fit_all_events_csi.png}
  \caption{Whole-plane depth profile of one reconstructed \SI{1}{\gray} run
    (points, Poisson errors)  with the erfc fit (Eq.~\ref{eq:erfc}, red line) in the
    fixed window (shaded). Left: BGO; right: CsI. The dashed guides
    mark the fitted plateau and its half; \Rc\ is the depth at which
    the fit crosses the half-height (dotted line, value in the legend).}
  \label{fig:fits}
\end{figure}

Notice that, for a given setup, the offset $\DR = \Roc - \Rc$ is fixed. A fit to
the data that determines \Rc, together with an estimate of \DR, therefore yields
\Roc\ and hence the proton distal edge. The uncertainty on \Roc\ can then be
expressed as


\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{\Roc} \;=\; \sigma_{\DR} \oplus \sigma_{\Rc},
  \label{eq:sigma_budget}
\end{equation}

The two terms are of a different nature. $\sigma_{\Rc}$ is obtained from the fit
to the data, so it is set by the errors on the measured activity profile $P(z)$.
These quantify the impact of the scanner---the sensitivity, which fixes the
available statistics; the energy resolution, which controls the effect of
multiple scattering; and the spatial resolution, which sets the precision with
which $P(z)$ is measured---together with radioactive decay and biological
washout, both of which reduce the activity with time. $\sigma_{\DR}$, in
contrast, quantifies the systematic error from an incomplete knowledge of the
setup: non-uniformities in the tissue that are not captured by the CT, the
imperfect knowledge of the nuclear cross sections, and the uncertainties in the
biological washout models, among others. For the past two decades the field has
worked to quantify and reduce these systematics, with a target of the order of
\SI{1}{mm}~\cite{Knopf2009,Paganetti2012}.

The target splits into two clinically distinct regimes. As a \emph{screening} tool---catching gross delivery errors such as a
wrong energy layer or a large anatomical change---a useful error is comparable to
the safety margin itself, of order $3$--$5$~mm, a level current PET systems already reach. 
The more demanding goal is \emph{margin
reduction}: the distal margin used in the clinic is
$M \simeq 3.5\%\times R + 1\,\mathrm{mm}$, about \SI{4.5}{mm} for a \SI{10}{cm}
range, and shrinking it safely calls for a total error at or below \SI{1}{mm}.  A useful criterion
is then to require $\sigma_{\Rc}$ to be negligible against the target,
$\sigma_{\Rc} \ll \SI{1}{mm}$, so that the useful error is limited by the
systematics rather than by the measurement. Driving $\sigma_{\Rc}$ well below the
millimetre is precisely the role of a high-sensitivity, high-resolution in-room
scanner, which then also becomes an instrument to isolate and, ultimately,
reduce $\sigma_{\DR}$ in a controlled setting. The remainder of this work
quantifies $\sigma_{\Rc}$ for the CsI and BGO scanners.

\section{Modelling biological washout}
\label{sec:washout}

The positron emitters produced along the beam path do not remain at their
production site. Perfusion and metabolism clear the radioactive atoms from the
irradiated volume, so the recorded activity is lower than physical decay alone
would predict. Following Mizuno et al.~\cite{Mizuno2003}, biological washout in
brain tissue is described by three exponential components (fast, medium and
slow), tentatively associated with direct blood flow, tissue--vessel exchange,
and trapping by stable molecules. The fraction of produced nuclei still present
a time $t$ after their creation is
%
\begin{equation}
  W(t) \;=\; \sum_{k=f,m,s} M_k\, e^{-\lambda_k^{\mathrm{bio}}\,t},
  \qquad \sum_{k} M_k = 1,
  \qquad \lambda_k^{\mathrm{bio}} = \frac{\ln 2}{T_k^{\mathrm{bio}}},
  \label{eq:washout}
\end{equation}
%
with the brain parameters $M_k$ and $T_k^{\mathrm{bio}}$ of
Table~\ref{tab:washout_brain}.

\begin{table}[ht]
  \centering
  \begin{tabular}{lccc}
    \hline
    Component & Fraction $M_k$ & $T_k^{\mathrm{bio}}$ (s) &
      $\lambda_k^{\mathrm{bio}}$ (s$^{-1}$) \\
    \hline
    Fast   & $0.35\,(3)$ & $2.0\,(1.8)$    & $3.5\times10^{-1}$ \\
    Medium & $0.30\,(3)$ & $140\,(18)$     & $5.0\times10^{-3}$ \\
    Slow   & $0.35\,(1)$ & $10191\,(2200)$ & $6.8\times10^{-5}$ \\
    \hline
  \end{tabular}
  \caption{Three-component biological washout parameters for brain tissue,
  from Mizuno et al.~\cite{Mizuno2003}. Uncertainties on the last digits are
  in parentheses.}
  \label{tab:washout_brain}
\end{table}

The simulated samples available to us model physical decay only, storing per species
$i$ the window-integrated signal without washout. Combining
Eq.~(\ref{eq:washout}) with the physical decay of species $i$ (rate
$\lambda_i^{\mathrm{phys}}$) and integrating over the acquisition window
$[t_1,t_2]$ opened after a uniform irradiation of duration $t_{\mathrm{irr}}$,
the combined effect of washout on the recorded signal of species $i$ reduces to a
single survival factor $g_i$, the fraction of physically recorded $i$-decays
that also survive clearance,
%
\begin{equation}
  g_i \;=\; \sum_{k=f,m,s} M_k\,
  \frac{\Phi\!\left(\lambda_i^{\mathrm{phys}}+\lambda_k^{\mathrm{bio}}\right)}
       {\Phi\!\left(\lambda_i^{\mathrm{phys}}\right)},
  \qquad
  \Phi(a) \;=\;
  \frac{\bigl(e^{a\,t_{\mathrm{irr}}}-1\bigr)
        \bigl(e^{-a t_1}-e^{-a t_2}\bigr)}{a^{2}} .
  \label{eq:gfactor}
\end{equation}
%
The production rate, spatial density and normalisation cancel in the ratio, so
$g_i$ is a pure number per isotope, and for a spatially uniform clearance it is
independent of position. 
Because each $g_i$ multiplies its species' entire depth profile $P_i(z)$ by a
constant, washout distorts no single isotope's shape and acts purely by
re-weighting the mixture,
%
\begin{equation}
  P^{\mathrm{wo}}(z) \;=\; \sum_i g_i\,P_i(z),
  \qquad
  P^{0}(z) \;=\; \sum_i P_i(z).
  \label{eq:mix}
\end{equation}
%
The parent Monte Carlo sample already includes isotope-dependent radioactive
decay. Biological washout is then applied as an additional survival
probability. Its effective window-integrated value $g_i$ depends on the isotope
because the common biological survival function is averaged over that
isotope's radioactive-decay time distribution.

The endpoint \Rc\ moves only insofar as the species occupy different depths.
$^{15}$O, with the lowest production threshold, reaches deepest and dominates the
distal edge, while $^{11}$C lies shallower. Every recorded nucleus is older than
the acquisition delay, well beyond the \SI{2}{\second} fast scale, so the fast component
is largely depleted for all species and washout roughly halves the signal.
Because $^{15}$O decays occur predominantly at shorter times, its contribution is
suppressed less by washout than that of $^{11}$C; the isotope component that
dominates the distal edge is therefore relatively preserved.



\section{Statistical treatment of the data: thinning}
\label{sec:thinning}

The range precision $\sigma_R$ is the run-to-run spread of \Rc\ at a given
dose and must therefore be estimated from an ensemble of acquisitions. We
generate this ensemble by Bernoulli thinning of ten statistically independent
Monte Carlo samples, each corresponding to a complete \SI{1}{\gray}
acquisition. The ten samples are pooled into a fixed master list,
and each event $e$ is retained independently with probability
%
\begin{equation}
  q_e = \frac{D}{n}\,s_e, \qquad
  s_e =
  \begin{cases}
    1, & \text{without biological washout},\\
    g_{i(e)}, & \text{with biological washout},
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:keep}
\end{equation}
%
where $D$ is the target dose in Gy, $n=10$ is the number of pooled
\SI{1}{\gray} samples, and $i(e)$ is the isotope label retained as Monte Carlo
truth. This label is used only to construct the simulated aggregate signal; a
PET measurement does not identify the isotope of an individual coincidence.
The acquisition window is already imposed in each input sample.

For each realisation, a uniform random number $u_e$ is generated for every
event and the event is retained when $u_e<q_e$. The nominal and washed samples
use the same $u_e$, so that the latter is a subset of the former. Each retained
list is reconstructed independently and fitted to obtain one \Rc. For $N$
realisations,
%
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_R^{\mathrm{thin}}
  = \mathrm{std}\bigl(R_{50}^{(1)},\ldots,R_{50}^{(N)}\bigr).
  \label{eq:sigmaR}
\end{equation}
%
Because all realisations reuse a fixed parent pool, their conditional count
variance is smaller than that of independent Poisson acquisitions. We correct
the measured spread by
%
\begin{equation}
 C_{\mathrm{pool}} =
 \left[\frac{\sum_e q_e}{\sum_e q_e(1-q_e)}\right]^{1/2},
 \qquad
 \sigma_R=C_{\mathrm{pool}}\,\sigma_R^{\mathrm{thin}}.
 \label{eq:poolcorrection}
\end{equation}
%
For a common probability $q$, this becomes $1/\sqrt{1-q}$; hence nominal
\SI{1}{\gray} thinning of the ten-sample pool has
$C_{\mathrm{pool}}=1/\sqrt{0.9}=1.054$. The washed correction is calculated
from the event-dependent probabilities in Eq.~(\ref{eq:keep}). The derivation,
fit-quality requirements and distinction between the finite-pool correction
and the finite-$N$ uncertainty are given in Appendix~\ref{app:thinning}.


\section{Statistical procedure}
\label{sec:statistical-procedure}

We illustrate and validate the complete procedure using the reference TBP
scanner with BGO at \SI{195}{\kelvin}, a \SI{1}{\gray} acquisition beginning
\SI{120}{s} after irradiation and ending at \SI{300}{s}; the acquisition
window is therefore \SI{180}{s}. First, the ten
statistically independent \SI{1}{\gray} Monte Carlo samples are reconstructed separately.
The erfc fits give a mean endpoint of \StatShardMean~mm and a direct sample-to-sample
spread $\sigma_R=\StatShardSigma$~mm. The individual fit uncertainties range
from \StatShardFitErrMin~mm to \StatShardFitErrMax~mm, and the median
$\chi^2/\mathrm{dof}$ is \StatShardChiMedian. The relative sampling uncertainty
of their standard deviation is
$1/\sqrt{2(10-1)}=23.6\%$.

We then pool the same samples and generate $N=100$ paired nominal and washed
realisations. The nominal distribution has mean \StatNomMean~mm and raw spread
$\sigma_R^{\mathrm{thin}}=\StatNomRaw$~mm. The nominal finite-pool factor is
$C_{\mathrm{pool}}=\StatNomCorrection$, giving the corrected
$\sigma_R=\StatNomCorrected$~mm. With washout, the corresponding mean is
\StatWashMean~mm, the raw spread is \StatWashRaw~mm, the event-dependent
correction is \StatWashCorrection, and the corrected spread is
\StatWashCorrected~mm. The median $\chi^2/\mathrm{dof}$ values are
\StatNomChiMedian\ and \StatWashChiMedian\ for the nominal and washed fits,
respectively; \StatNomFailures\ nominal and \StatWashFailures\ washed fits
fail the quality requirements. With $N=100$, the relative sampling uncertainty
on either standard deviation is $1/\sqrt{2(100-1)}=7.1\%$. The
$\chi^2/\mathrm{dof}$ values exceed unity systematically, so they are retained
as fit diagnostics rather than interpreted as a literal goodness-of-fit test
for statistically independent profile bins.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclu}

CRYSP scanners provide an alternative to in-beam systems that combines full-ring geometry and high sensitivity with good energy and spatial resolution at comparatively low detector cost. Their use affords short scans that minimise the impact of biological washout~\cite{Mizuno2003} and patient movement~\cite{Knopf2009}. The statistical uncertainty associated with the scanner response, radioactive decay and the modelled washout is much smaller than the target precision for distal-edge prediction. Several configurations are possible, depending on the application and the budget. Among the configurations studied, the BGO LAFOV combines greater anatomical coverage with lower $\sigma_R$, whereas the CsI CAFOV provides a lower-cost option for head and other well-localised tumours. These scanners could support systematic studies of the uncertainties affecting distal-edge prediction and, ultimately, clinical range verification.

\appendix
\section{Statistical basis and validation of the thinning method}
\label{app:thinning}

\subsection{Parent sample and Bernoulli thinning}

Let the parent sample contain $M$ detected coincidences obtained by pooling
$n$ independent Monte Carlo samples. Each sample is generated with a different Monte Carlo
seed and represents the complete acquisition at the reference dose. Isotope
production, physical radioactive decay, the acquisition window and detector
response are therefore already represented in the parent events. For a target
dose $D$, event $e$ is retained when
%
\begin{equation}
  X_e = \mathbf{1}(u_e<q_e), \qquad
  u_e\sim\mathcal{U}[0,1), \qquad
  q_e=\frac{D}{n}s_e .
  \label{eq:appendix-keep}
\end{equation}
%
The retained count is $K=\sum_e X_e$. Independent uniform draws are used for
different realisations. Within one realisation, the same $u_e$ is used for the
nominal selection ($s_e=1$) and the washed selection
($s_e=g_{i(e)}\leq1$). Consequently every washed list is a subset of its
nominal partner. This pairing removes avoidable Monte Carlo noise when their
means or spreads are compared.

\subsection{Physical decay and biological washout}

The biological survival law $W(t)$ in Eq.~(\ref{eq:washout}) is common to all
isotopes in the adopted uniform brain model. It is not an isotope-identification
model. Starting from a produced nucleus of species $i$, the time dependence of
the recorded signal contains both physical decay and biological survival,
%
\begin{equation}
  A_i^{\mathrm{wo}}(t) \propto
  \lambda_i^{\mathrm{phys}}
  e^{-\lambda_i^{\mathrm{phys}}t} W(t),
  \qquad
  A_i^0(t) \propto
  \lambda_i^{\mathrm{phys}}
  e^{-\lambda_i^{\mathrm{phys}}t}.
  \label{eq:decay-washout}
\end{equation}
%
The simulated parent sample already contains the second of these time laws, so
physical decay must not be applied again during thinning. The additional factor
$g_i$ is the ratio of the signal integrated over irradiation and acquisition
times with washout to the corresponding signal with physical decay alone,
%
\begin{equation}
  g_i =
  \frac{\displaystyle\int_{\mathrm{window}}
        A_i^0(t)W(t)\,\mathrm{d}t}
       {\displaystyle\int_{\mathrm{window}}
        A_i^0(t)\,\mathrm{d}t},
  \label{eq:gi-ratio}
\end{equation}
%
with the irradiation-time integration understood; its closed form is
Eq.~(\ref{eq:gfactor}). Thus biological clearance is common, while its
effective window average differs between isotopes because their physical-decay
time distributions differ. A shorter-lived isotope weights earlier times,
when less clearance has occurred.

The production Monte Carlo records the latent isotope label $i(e)$ for each
coincidence. Assigning $s_e=g_{i(e)}$ uses that truth information to generate
the expected summed PET signal. It does not imply that the scanner identifies
the isotope: isotope labels are discarded by the reconstruction, which receives
one combined list of retained LORs. A microscopic simulation storing the age of
every nucleus could instead retain event $e$ with probability proportional to
$W(t_e)$; the present method analytically averages this survival over the
species-dependent physical-decay distribution.

\subsection{Conditional and Poisson count variances}

If the parent process were generated anew for every acquisition, independent
thinning of a Poisson process would give a Poisson retained count. Conditional
on the one realised parent pool used here, however, each $X_e$ is Bernoulli.
For event-dependent probabilities,
%
\begin{align}
  \mathrm{E}[K\mid\mathrm{pool}] &= \sum_e q_e, \\
  \mathrm{Var}[K\mid\mathrm{pool}] &= \sum_e q_e(1-q_e).
  \label{eq:conditional-counts}
\end{align}
%
An independently generated Poisson acquisition with the same expected signal
has
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Var}_{\mathrm{Poisson}}[K]=\sum_e q_e.
  \label{eq:poisson-counts}
\end{equation}
%
The conditional ensemble therefore lacks the fluctuation that generated the
finite parent pool. The ratio of the Poisson and conditional count standard
deviations gives Eq.~(\ref{eq:poolcorrection}). When $q_e=q$ for all events,
%
\begin{equation}
  C_{\mathrm{pool}} =
  \sqrt{\frac{Mq}{Mq(1-q)}}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-q}}.
  \label{eq:uniform-pool-correction}
\end{equation}
%
For nominal \SI{1}{\gray} thinning of ten pooled \SI{1}{\gray} samples,
$q=0.1$ and $C_{\mathrm{pool}}=1.054$. With washout,
$q_e=0.1g_{i(e)}$ is smaller and varies between species, so the general sum in
Eq.~(\ref{eq:poolcorrection}) is used. The washed correction can be smaller
than 1.054 even though washout increases $\sigma_R$: the reduced $q_e$ makes
reuse of any particular parent event less important, while the lower retained
count still degrades the endpoint precision.

The variance ratio is exact for event counts. Its use for the reconstructed
endpoint is tested directly in Sec.~\ref{sec:statistical-procedure}: the
finite-pool-corrected thinning spread is compared with the spread of endpoints
from the independently generated simulations.

\subsection{Finite-ensemble precision}

The finite-pool correction and the sampling uncertainty on a measured standard
deviation answer different questions. For $N$ independent Gaussian endpoint
values, the relative standard error of their sample standard deviation is
approximately
%
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\delta\sigma_R}{\sigma_R}
  \simeq \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(N-1)}}.
  \label{eq:sigma-sampling-error}
\end{equation}
%
It is 23.6\% for the ten independent simulations, 7.1\% for $N=100$, and 5.0\%
for $N=200$. Increasing $N$ determines the conditional thinning distribution
more precisely but does not create new parent Monte Carlo samples. The
multiplicative $C_{\mathrm{pool}}$ restores the missing count variance; the
quantity in Eq.~(\ref{eq:sigma-sampling-error}) describes how precisely the
corrected spread is estimated from a finite number of endpoints.

\subsection{Endpoint-fit quality}

Each reconstructed depth profile is fitted with a free-baseline erfc model.
The baseline and amplitude are constrained to be non-negative, \Rc\ is
constrained to the predefined distal fit window, and the positive edge-width
parameter is bounded by that window. Non-convergent fits, non-finite parameters
or covariance matrices, and solutions outside these physical bounds are marked
as failures. The $\chi^2/\mathrm{dof}$ of every accepted fit is retained as a
diagnostic. For an ensemble of $N$ endpoints, the analysis is rejected if the
number of failed fits exceeds
%
\begin{equation}
  n_{\mathrm{fail,max}}=\left\lfloor0.05N\right\rfloor .
  \label{eq:fit-failure-limit}
\end{equation}
%
The endpoint distribution is inspected in addition to these per-fit checks;
the Gaussian overlays in Fig.~\ref{fig:statistical-procedure} are descriptive
and are not used to determine \Rc\ or $\sigma_R$.
